Introduction

What makes a recipe earn five stars? This project investigates one specific angle: does the amount of sugar in a recipe affect how users rate it? Using the Food.com Recipes & Ratings dataset, I explore whether recipes above the median sugar level receive meaningfully different ratings than lower-sugar ones — and what that reveals about how platform users evaluate food.

My central question is: "Does the amount of sugar in a recipe affect how users rate it?" Specifically, do high-sugar recipes receive meaningfully different average ratings than low-sugar ones? This matters because it can reveal whether users on a recipe platform reward indulgent, high-sugar dishes or whether sugar content has no bearing on satisfaction — a question relevant to both recipe creators and platform designers.

The dataset is sourced from Food.com and consists of two files: RAW_recipes.csv containing 83,782 unique recipes, and interactions.csv containing 731,927 user–recipe interactions (ratings and reviews). These were left-merged on recipe ID so every recipe is retained even if it has no reviews. Ratings of 0 were replaced with NaN before computing per-recipe averages, since a 0 indicates a missing interaction rather than a genuine score.

The key columns relevant to this analysis are: id (unique recipe identifier), minutes (total prep time in minutes), n_steps (number of recipe steps), nutrition (a list string parsed into 7 numeric fields: calories, total fat, sugar, sodium, protein, saturated fat, carbohydrates), description (free-text written by the contributor, has non-trivial missingness), and average_rating (mean of all user ratings for that recipe, derived from the interactions file).

How Are Recipes Rated?

The rating distribution tells an immediate story: users overwhelmingly award 5 stars. Over 50,000 recipes sit at exactly 5.0, while ratings below 4 are very rare. This strong positive skew is typical of recipe platforms — users mostly rate dishes they genuinely enjoyed. The compression toward 5.0 is the central challenge for prediction: there is very little variance for any model to exploit.

How Are Calories Distributed?

Filtered to recipes under 2,000 calories, the distribution is right-skewed with a peak around 150–300 calories per serving. The long tail toward higher calorie counts reflects indulgent dishes such as cakes and casseroles. This wide spread provides useful variance for the calories_per_minute feature engineered in the final model.

Does Complexity Affect Ratings?

The box plot shows average ratings grouped by step count (1–30). Median ratings stay flat near 5.0 at every complexity level and interquartile ranges barely shift, indicating that step count alone has no meaningful association with user satisfaction. This motivates combining step count with time information via the steps_per_minute feature rather than using it in isolation.

Despite spanning recipes with 1 to 30 steps, median ratings remain near 5.0 throughout and the interquartile ranges are nearly identical. This flat trend confirms that recipe complexity alone does not predict user satisfaction.

Assessment of Missingness

Missing data can introduce hidden biases. I examined which columns have missing values and tested whether those gaps are random or systematically related to other variables.

Is Missingness in Ratings NMAR?

The rating column is likely Missing Not At Random (NMAR). The decision to leave a rating is tied directly to the unobserved outcome — a user's experience with the recipe. Users who felt strongly (loved or hated it) are far more likely to rate than users who felt neutral. Because the missingness mechanism depends on the missing value itself (the unobserved experience), no observed column can fully predict it. To make this MAR, we would need additional data such as whether a user viewed the recipe page without submitting a rating.

Extreme reactions (love it / hate it) prompt reviews. Indifference does not. Missing ratings likely skew observed averages upward, since neutral experiences go unrecorded.

Missingness Dependency Tests

Note: the NMAR argument above applies to the rating column. The permutation tests below analyze a separate column — description — which also has non-trivial missingness and is well-suited for testing MAR vs. MCAR since its absence may plausibly depend on observable recipe attributes.

I ran permutation tests for description missingness against seven candidate variables using the absolute difference in group means as the test statistic and α = 0.05. Description missingness does depend on submitted_year (p = 0.010) and protein (p = 0.027) — making it MAR with respect to those columns. It does not depend on cooking time, calories, sugar, carbohydrates, or step count — making it effectively MCAR with respect to those variables.

Hypothesis Testing

The grouped table in the EDA section hinted that high-sugar recipes have a marginally higher average rating (4.63 vs 4.62). But is this difference real, or just noise? A formal permutation test decides.

H0: The mean average rating is the same for high-sugar and low-sugar recipes.

H1: The mean average rating differs between high-sugar and low-sugar recipes.

With a p-value of 0.2610, well above 0.05, we cannot conclude that sugar content is associated with rating differences. The null distribution below shows the observed gap is entirely consistent with random chance.

There is no statistically significant evidence that high-sugar recipes are rated differently from low-sugar ones. We fail to reject the null — this does not prove sugar has no effect, only that any effect is too small to detect with this data and test.

Framing a Prediction Problem

The hypothesis test showed sugar alone cannot explain ratings. But what if we combine many features? I frame this as a regression problem: predict a recipe's average_rating — a continuous variable ranging from 1.0 to 5.0 stars — from features available at the time the recipe is posted. Regression is the appropriate choice here because average_rating is a continuous numeric target with meaningful distances between values (a 4.0 and a 4.5 are genuinely different). Classification would require arbitrarily bucketing ratings into categories, losing information and making the task harder to interpret.

I chose RMSE as the evaluation metric for three reasons: (1) it is expressed in the same units as ratings (stars), making results directly interpretable to a reader — an RMSE of 0.64 means predictions are off by about two-thirds of a star on average; (2) it penalizes larger errors more heavily than MAE, which is appropriate here since a prediction that is 2 stars wrong is far more damaging than one that is 0.2 stars wrong; (3) unlike R², RMSE gives an absolute measure of error rather than a relative one — R² would be misleadingly low here simply because the rating distribution has low variance (most recipes are near 5.0), not because the model is poor. To prevent data leakage, I only use features known at recipe post time — minutes, n_steps, calories, sugar, protein — never any information derived from future user interactions.

Baseline Model

My baseline pipeline uses three features, implemented end-to-end in a single sklearn Pipeline:

  • minutes — quantitative; standardized with StandardScaler to put it on the same scale as n_steps
  • n_steps — quantitative; standardized with StandardScaler
  • is_high_sugar — nominal (binary flag above median sugar); one-hot encoded with OneHotEncoder

Feature count by type: 2 quantitative, 0 ordinal, 1 nominal. The pipeline was fit on an 80/20 train-test split using LinearRegression.

Evaluated on the held-out test set (20% of data, unseen during training), the baseline achieves an RMSE of 0.6359 — predictions are off by roughly two-thirds of a star on average. Given that over 60% of recipes are rated exactly 5.0, this is a reasonable starting point, but the model is not "good" in any practical sense: the linear model cannot capture non-linear interactions, and the three features are weak individual predictors. Note also that StandardScaler on a linear regression changes only the coefficients, not the predictions — it was applied here for numerical stability and comparability, not to improve RMSE. There is clear room to improve through feature engineering and a more expressive model.

Final Model

To improve on the baseline, I engineered two new features:

  • calories_per_minute — captures caloric density relative to cooking effort. From a data-generating-process perspective, a 600-calorie recipe that takes 10 minutes is a fundamentally different type of dish (quick indulgence) than a 600-calorie recipe that takes 90 minutes (laborious project). Users may rate these categories differently, and neither calories nor minutes alone captures this distinction.
  • steps_per_minute — a proxy for recipe intensity per unit time. A recipe with many steps relative to its cooking time demands more active involvement from the cook. Users who find a recipe more demanding than expected may rate it lower, while users who find it surprisingly efficient may rate it higher. This ratio captures that complexity-per-effort dimension that neither n_steps nor minutes encodes individually.

I evaluated two gradient boosting families using 5-fold cross-validation with GridSearchCV, searching over learning_rate (controls step size — smaller values generalise better but need more trees), max_depth (controls tree complexity — shallow trees reduce overfitting on noisy rating data), n_estimators (number of boosting rounds), and subsample (fraction of data per tree, adds stochasticity to reduce variance). These four hyperparameters were chosen because they are the primary drivers of the bias-variance tradeoff in gradient boosting. Both GradientBoostingRegressor and HistGradientBoostingRegressor were evaluated; the standard GradientBoostingRegressor achieved lower CV RMSE and was selected as the final model.

The improvement from 0.6359 to 0.6354 RMSE is modest but expected: average ratings are extremely compressed near 5.0, so there is inherently little signal for any model to exploit. The gradient boosting model generalises well (CV RMSE ≈ test RMSE, ruling out overfitting), and the marginal improvement reflects a genuine constraint of the dataset rather than a modelling shortcoming. Compared to the baseline Linear Regression (RMSE = 0.6359), the final Gradient Boosting model achieves RMSE = 0.6354 on the same test set — an improvement of 0.0005 stars. The engineered features do help — a model without them achieves a higher RMSE (i.e., performs worse).

The model is not equally accurate across sugar groups. High-sugar recipes (RMSE = 0.654) are harder to predict than low-sugar ones (RMSE = 0.617) — a gap of 0.037 stars. In practical terms, this means predictions for high-sugar recipes are about 6% less accurate in absolute star terms than for low-sugar ones, which is a meaningful disparity given the narrow range of ratings in this dataset. This likely reflects that high-sugar recipes (desserts, baked goods) have more variable ratings — they tend to be more polarising — making them inherently harder to predict. Future work should explore additional predictors or group-specific calibration to address this gap.
